## Topic 1: Foundations of Entrepreneurship

### 1.1 What is entrepreneurship?
There is no single accepted definition. At its most basic, entrepreneurship is **the process of identifying opportunities and creating value from them**.

Four key theories to know:

| Theory | Author (Year) | Main idea |
|---|---|---|
| **Alertness theory** | Kirzner (1973) | The entrepreneur is an *alert* person who notices gaps (disequilibria) in the market and acts on them. Profit is the reward for *discovery*. |
| **Creative destruction** | Schumpeter (1934) | The entrepreneur is an *innovator* who disrupts existing markets with **new combinations** — new products, new production methods, new markets, new forms of organisation. A change agent that drives long-run growth. |
| **Effectuation theory** | Sarasvathy (2001) | Expert entrepreneurs do not start from a fixed goal (causation). They start from **available means** — who I am, what I know, whom I know — and ask what can be created from them. |
| **Opportunity–individual nexus** | Shane & Venkataraman (2000) | Entrepreneurship research studies the meeting point (nexus) of enterprising individuals and valuable opportunities — how opportunities are recognised, evaluated, and exploited. |

**Important distinction:** not every small business owner is an entrepreneur. A roadside food vendor who copies an existing model is *self-employed*, not an entrepreneur in the Schumpeterian (innovating) sense.

### 1.2 Historical evolution
- Entrepreneurship is **not a modern invention**. Pre-colonial West African long-distance trade (Hausa, Yoruba, Igbo merchants on Saharan and Atlantic routes) was entrepreneurial organisation centuries before the term existed. Medieval Islamic traders used *commenda* contracts — early risk-sharing arrangements (Goitein, 1967).
- The **Industrial Revolution** (c. 1760–1840) created conditions for large-scale enterprise (mechanisation, the factory system, capital accumulation).
- The twentieth century institutionalised entrepreneurship inside corporations — Chandler (1977) called this the "visible hand" of managerial capitalism.
- Recent decades: the knowledge economy and venture-backed startups (Silicon Valley). In Africa in the 2010s: **"Silicon Savannah"** (Nairobi), **"Yabacon Valley"** (Lagos), **"Silicon Cape"** (Cape Town) — driven by mobile penetration, a youthful population, and diaspora investment. Nigerian examples: **Flutterwave** (fintech unicorn) and **Andela** (human capital).
- **Critical point:** the Silicon Valley startup story may not fit Nigeria. Much Nigerian entrepreneurship is **necessity entrepreneurship** (survivalist, informal, driven by unemployment), not **opportunity entrepreneurship** (Audretsch et al., 2006). Both are legitimate but need different policies.

### 1.3 The entrepreneurial mindset: evidence and myths
- The **trait approach** (the "born risk-taker", the "lone genius") is largely **discredited**. Traits like need for achievement (McClelland, 1961), locus of control, and tolerance for ambiguity correlate with entrepreneurial intent, but they are **neither necessary nor sufficient** for success.
- **Structural factors** — access to finance, networks, institutions, market access, education — matter at least as much as personality.
- **Ajzen's (1991) Theory of Planned Behaviour**: intention to start a venture is predicted by (1) attitude toward entrepreneurship, (2) subjective norms, and (3) perceived behavioural control. Because intentions can be shaped, **entrepreneurship is teachable**.
- "Born or made?" — the balanced answer: genetics may create propensities, but context, education, exposure, and institutions decide whether propensity becomes action. Example: the **Tony Elumelu Foundation (TEEP)** trains and funds 5,000 African entrepreneurs each year on the premise that entrepreneurship can be cultivated.

### 1.4 Economic and social impact
- New ventures create **employment** directly and through supply chains; innovation raises productivity and long-run growth (Audretsch & Thurik, 2001).
- Nigeria: **MSMEs account for about 96% of businesses and 84% of employment** (National Bureau of Statistics, 2021).
- **Social entrepreneurship** uses market mechanisms to solve social problems — e.g. **Co-creation Hub (CcHUB)** in Lagos; the **Grameen Bank** microcredit model (Yunus, 2007, Nobel Peace Prize).
- **Caution:** entrepreneurship is not automatically good for development (Wennekers & Thurik, 1999). High self-employment in poor countries often reflects **labour market failure**, not vibrant opportunity.

### 1.5 Case studies
- **Aliko Dangote (Dangote Group, est. 1977):** started in commodity trading (sugar, cement, flour), then integrated vertically into manufacturing behind high tariffs (import substitution). Dangote Cement operates in fourteen African countries; the Dangote Refinery (2024) is the world's largest single-train refinery. *Critical point:* his success is inseparable from state–business relations (import licences, favourable policies, state finance). Debate: Schumpeterian entrepreneur or rent-seeking oligopolist?
- **Flutterwave (2016, Olugbenga Agboola & Iyinoluwa Aboyeji):** built payment infrastructure to fix Africa's fragmented payment systems; by 2023 had processed 400m+ transactions worth over $25bn; unicorn status. *Critical point:* success depended on ecosystem factors — Lagos talent, telecoms, a large unbanked population, and CBN regulatory innovation.

## Topic 2: Opportunity Recognition, Feasibility, and Business Models

### 2.1 Opportunity recognition
- (First page missing from scan; the readings show it draws on **Alvarez & Barney (2007)** — opportunities can be *discovered* (they exist already, waiting to be found) or *created* (they come into being through entrepreneurial action).)
- **Prior knowledge and the knowledge corridor (Shane, 2000):** people recognise opportunities that match their prior knowledge. Shane studied eight people who saw the same MIT invention (3-D printing); each saw a different commercial use depending on their industry experience. Lesson: **diverse, cross-functional founding teams** see more opportunities.

### 2.2 Feasibility analysis
- Feasibility analysis = the systematic assessment of whether an identified opportunity can become a sustainable venture. Four parts (Burns, 2018): **market, technical, financial, and organisational feasibility**.
- In Nigeria, reliable secondary data is scarce, so **primary research** (surveys, interviews, observation) is very important. Useful secondary sources: National Bureau of Statistics, CBN Economic Report.
- **SWOT analysis** (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) is a useful starting structure, but it is criticised for producing long lists without priorities (Dyson, 2004). The **TOWS matrix** (matching internal factors to external ones) is more rigorous.

### 2.3 Business models
- A **business model** describes **how an organisation creates, delivers, and captures value** (Teece, 2010). It is *not* the same as strategy (competitive positioning) or a business plan (a document).
- A sound business model is **necessary but not sufficient** — it must also be hard to imitate and aligned with strategy.

Business model types relevant to Nigeria:

| Model | Meaning | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| **Subscription** | Recurring payment for ongoing access | Netflix; Konga Prime |
| **Platform / marketplace** | Matches two or more user groups; network effects; "cold start" problem | Uber, Airbnb; Bolt, Jumia |
| **Freemium** | Basic free, premium paid; needs high conversion | Spotify, LinkedIn |
| **Social enterprise** | Commercial revenue + social mission; risk of mission drift | CcHUB, Kuli Kuli |
| **Informal / trade** | Dominant model in Nigeria; outside formal registration; built on trust networks | Most Nigerian micro-businesses |

- **Critical lens — Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder & Pigneur, 2010):** the most used tool in entrepreneurship education, but critics note business models are "calculative devices" that *shape* what the entrepreneur sees, and the canvas is **descriptive and static** (Foss & Saebi, 2017). Use it as a thinking scaffold, not a blueprint.
- **Airbnb case (2008):** did not discover a market — it **created** one, starting from personal necessity (renting air mattresses) and iterative experimentation. Its success depended on structural enablers: the 2008 financial crisis, digital payments (Stripe), and trust via user reviews. It also brought controversy (housing markets, regulation, tax).

## Topic 3: Innovation and Creativity in Practice

### 3.1 What is innovation?
- **Invention** = creating a new idea or artefact. **Creativity** = the mental capacity that generates novel associations. **Innovation** = the successful commercialisation or social deployment of new ideas (Tidd & Bessant, 2021). A patented compound that never reaches the market is an invention, not an innovation.
- **Oslo Manual (OECD/Eurostat, 2018)** — four types of innovation: **product, process, marketing, organisational**.
- **Christensen (1997) — disruptive innovation:** *sustaining* innovations improve existing products for existing customers; *disruptive* innovations start out worse on mainstream measures but win by serving overlooked segments or creating new markets. Example: **M-Pesa** disrupted formal banking in East Africa by serving the unbanked.
- **Critique:** King & Baatartogtokh (2015) found fewer than 10% of Christensen's own 77 cases were clear disruption; Lepore (2014) argued the cases were selectively interpreted. Use the theory critically, not as a law.

### 3.2 Creativity techniques and their evidence
- **Brainstorming** (Osborn, 1953): research shows *nominal groups* (individuals working separately) usually produce more and better ideas than interacting groups — because of production blocking, evaluation apprehension, and social loafing (Diehl & Stroebe, 1987). Electronic brainstorming partly fixes this.
- **Design Thinking** (Brown, 2008; Stanford d.school): five stages — **Empathise, Define, Ideate, Prototype, Test**. Its key contribution: deep empathy with users *before* generating ideas, so teams do not solve the wrong problem.
- **Lateral thinking** (de Bono, 1970): deliberately introduces random stimuli or reverses assumptions. Popular in practice but has limited peer-reviewed validation — treat as a heuristic.

### 3.3 Technology as an innovation driver (African context)
- Africa's digital transformation is **mobile-first**: mobile internet *leapfrogged* fixed-line infrastructure entirely.
- Nigerian fintech: **OPay, PalmPay, Kuda Bank** reached tens of millions of users in a country with a large unbanked population. The **CBN regulatory sandbox (2021)** allows controlled fintech experimentation.
- AI, blockchain and IoT are increasingly relevant: AI decision support in agriculture (Farmcrowdy), blockchain land registry pilots (Ghana, Kenya), IoT irrigation — genuine innovations adapted to African contexts.

### 3.4 Barriers to innovation
Three levels — a common exam framing:
1. **Individual:** fear of failure (amplified by Nigeria's high-uncertainty economy); cognitive biases like status quo bias and loss aversion (Kahneman, 2011).
2. **Organisational:** **structural inertia** (Hannan & Freeman, 1984) — successful organisations build routines optimised for existing products, which is why disruption usually comes from new entrants.
3. **Systemic:** weak intellectual property regimes, limited R&D finance, poor infrastructure, weak university–industry links. The National Innovation Policy (2012) exists but implementation is inconsistent.

### 3.5 Case study: M-Pesa (Safaricom, Kenya, 2007)
- Mobile money transfer and payment; included millions of Kenyans financially within a few years. Jack & Suri (2016, *Science*) found M-Pesa access lifted ~2% of Kenyan households out of poverty, particularly women-headed households.
- M-Pesa was **not invented in a lab** — it was co-created through field deployment, user feedback, and negotiation with the Central Bank of Kenya. It illustrates **effectuation** (starting from available means — Safaricom's subscriber base) and **frugal innovation** (Radjou & Prabhu, 2015): doing more with less, for more people, in resource-constrained settings.

## Topic 4: Entrepreneurial Finance and Resource Management

### 4.1 Sources of finance (theory + Nigerian realities)
- Access to finance is the **principal barrier** reported by Nigerian MSMEs (World Bank Enterprise Survey, 2023).
- **Pecking order theory (Myers & Majluf, 1984):** entrepreneurs prefer internal financing first (bootstrapping), then debt, then external equity — to avoid information-asymmetry costs and keep control. This matches Nigerian behaviour: heavy reliance on personal savings and **ROSCAs** (rotating savings groups — *ajo* / *esusu*) before formal finance.
- Formal options:
  - **Microfinance institutions** — key for micro-enterprises, but interest rates often exceed 30% per annum (debt burden risk).
  - **Development finance** — Bank of Industry (BOI), Development Bank of Nigeria (DBN), CBN Targeted Credit Facility; concessionary rates but require formalisation and documentation.
  - **Angel investment & venture capital** — growing but concentrated in Lagos/Abuja and in tech (Lagos Angel Network, Microtraction; AfricInvest, TLcom).
  - **Crowdfunding** — Kickstarter (global), Thundafund (Africa-focused); good for creative and social enterprises.
  - **Grants** — Tony Elumelu Foundation ($5,000 seed grants), AfDB, donors; non-dilutive but competitive and slow.
- **Critical lens — the collateral trap:** formal lending in Nigeria requires collateral, usually landed property. Because land tenure is insecure and registration bureaucratic, this **structurally excludes** many would-be entrepreneurs — especially women, youth, and rural dwellers. De Soto (2000): formalising property rights would unlock "dead capital" (though its fit to Nigeria is contested).

### 4.2 Financial literacy — core concepts
- **Cash flow:** the timing difference between cash received and cash paid. **A profitable business can still fail** if cash inflows consistently lag outflows. Manage debtor days, supplier credit, and keep a cash buffer.
- **Break-even analysis:** the revenue level where total costs = total revenues. Formula: **Break-even units = Fixed Costs ÷ (Selling Price per unit − Variable Cost per unit)**.
- **Profit margin:** net profit ÷ revenue, as a percentage. Gross margin excludes overheads; net margin includes all costs.
- **Working capital:** current assets − current liabilities. Negative working capital signals near-term liquidity risk.
- **Accrual vs cash accounting:** accrual records revenue/expenses when *earned or incurred*; cash accounting records them when *cash moves*. Nigerian micro-enterprises mostly use cash accounting; accrual matters when preparing statements for investors or lenders.

### 4.3 The Resource-Based View (RBV)
- **Barney (1991):** sustained competitive advantage comes from resources that are **Valuable, Rare, Inimitable, Non-substitutable (VRIN)**. Entrepreneurs should build distinctive resource combinations, not just imitate incumbents.
- **Human capital** (Becker, 1964): the founding team's skills and experience — often the most critical early resource. General skills vs firm-specific skills.
- **Social capital** (Bourdieu, 1986): networks, trust, shared norms. Especially important in Nigeria, where institutional trust is low and personal relationships underpin transactions. Entrepreneurial success often means converting social capital into economic capital (contracts, referrals, credit).

### 4.4 Risk management
- Categories of risk: **market** (customers may not buy), **execution** (team may fail to deliver), **financial** (cash may run out), **regulatory** (rules may change), and — especially in Nigeria — **macroeconomic** (inflation, exchange-rate volatility, fuel scarcity).
- Process: **risk identification → risk assessment (probability × impact) → risk response (avoid, transfer, mitigate, accept) → monitoring**.
- Insurance as a risk-transfer tool is undersupplied in Nigeria: insurance penetration is about **0.3% of GDP vs ~7% global average** — both a challenge and a market opportunity.

## Topic 5: Career Development and Employability Skills

### 5.1 Career development theories
- **Super (1980) — Life-Span, Life-Space theory:** career development is an ongoing process of developing and implementing a self-concept through five stages: **Growth, Exploration, Establishment, Maintenance, Decline/Disengagement**. Undergraduates are typically in **Exploration** (ages ~15–24): crystallising, specifying, implementing occupational preferences.
- **Holland (1997) — RIASEC:** six vocational personality/work-environment types: **Realistic, Investigative, Artistic, Social, Enterprising, Conventional**. People whose personality matches their environment report greater satisfaction and persistence. Assessment tool: the Self-Directed Search (SDS), validated in African settings (Watson et al., 2002).
- **Critical lens:** these are Western, individualistic theories. African scholars (Mkhize, 2003; Stead & Watson, 2017) note that **Ubuntu-influenced collectivist values** — where career decisions reflect family and community obligations — are not well captured. Apply with cultural reflexivity.

### 5.2 What do employers actually want?
- **USEM model (Knight & Yorke, 2004):** employability = **U**nderstanding, **S**kilful practices, **E**fficacy beliefs, **M**etacognition. Generic capabilities (communication, teamwork, problem-solving, digital literacy) are systematically underweighted by universities relative to employer demand.
- Nigeria: Agabi & Ogbondah (2014) documented a mismatch — employers cite deficits in **written communication, quantitative reasoning, professional conduct, and initiative** more than gaps in subject knowledge.
- **Digital literacy is now a threshold requirement:** data tools (Excel, SPSS, Python), communication platforms (Teams, Slack), project tools (Asana, Trello), presentation skills. COVID-19 normalised remote/hybrid work.

### 5.3 Personal career planning
- Steps: honest self-assessment → environmental scanning → goal-setting → action planning.
- **Personal SWOT**: transferable strengths, developmental weaknesses, external opportunities (growth sectors, in-demand skills), threats (automation, graduate oversupply).
- **SMART goals** (Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Relevant, Time-bound) — but given the uncertain Nigerian labour market, **Savickas (2011) Career Construction Theory** (adaptive coping, career identity, narrative self-making) may be more realistic: aim for **"direction plus flexibility"**, not rigid maps.

### 5.4 Professional networking in Nigeria
- Many Nigerian positions are filled through **referral and recommendation**, not open advertisement.
- **Granovetter (1973) — "the strength of weak ties":** opportunities come more often through *acquaintances* than close friends, because acquaintances carry non-redundant information from other social circles.
- Channels: professional associations (NBA, ICAN, CIPM), alumni networks (LASU Alumni Association), digital platforms (LinkedIn — 6m+ Nigerian users by 2024; Jobberman).
- Approach networking **relationally** (what can I contribute?) not transactionally (what can I get?).

## Topic 6: Entrepreneurship and Innovation in Career Growth

### 6.1 Intrapreneurship (entrepreneurial action inside organisations)
- Theorised by **Pinchot (1985)**. **Antoncic & Hisrich (2001)** define corporate entrepreneurship (CE) along four dimensions: **new business venturing, innovativeness, self-renewal, proactiveness** — entrepreneurial behaviour without bearing full personal financial risk.
- **Zahra (1991)** showed CE activities improve financial performance in listed firms.
- Nigerian examples: Access Bank's AccessLabs, MTN's Ayoba, Zenith Bank's digital transformation — though implementation faces organisational resistance, resource hoarding, and misaligned incentives.
- **Hornsby et al. (2002) — five enablers of intrapreneurship:** management support, autonomy/work discretion, rewards for innovation, resources (incl. time), and supportive organisational boundaries. Intrapreneurship needs *structural conditions*, not just individual initiative.
- **Case — Google's "20% time":** often cited as producing Gmail and Google Maps, but this is largely myth — most such products were resourced as formal projects, and engineers under delivery pressure rarely used the 20% option (Mayer, 2012). Lesson: judge an organisation's innovation culture by **substantive** enablers (reduced workloads, real autonomy, ring-fenced resources, tolerance of failure), not rhetoric.

### 6.2 Entrepreneurial mindset as career capital
- Components: initiative, opportunity orientation, risk tolerance, resilience, creative problem-solving. Employers value these across sectors — a form of cultural capital (Bourdieu, 1986) convertible into employment advantage.
- **Psychological Capital (PsyCap)** — Luthans et al. (2007): **hope, efficacy, resilience, optimism** — predicts employee performance and satisfaction.
- **Caution:** not all workers can take equal risks. Family dependants, debt, or precarious status change the risk calculus. The mindset is valuable but its use is **structurally shaped**.

### 6.3 Innovation as a career differentiator
- **Frey & Osborne (2013):** ~47% of US occupations at high automation risk; African labour markets may be even more exposed (World Bank, 2016) because employment is concentrated in routine agricultural and manufacturing roles.
- Staying relevant requires continuous learning — **Dweck (2006) growth mindset:** abilities can be developed through effort; people who attribute setbacks to insufficient *effort* rather than insufficient *ability* persist and improve more.

## Topic 7: Ethics, Sustainability, and the Future of Work

### 7.1 Ethical frameworks for entrepreneurs
| Framework | Source | Core idea |
|---|---|---|
| **Deontological** | Kant (1785) | Actions are right or wrong in themselves. Never deceive customers even if profitable — people are ends, not means. Relevant to honest marketing, contracts, disclosure. |
| **Utilitarian** | Mill (1863) | Maximise aggregate welfare; dominant logic in cost–benefit analysis — but can licence sacrificing minority interests. |
| **Virtue ethics** | Aristotle | Focus on the character of the person: honesty, integrity, prudence, justice as habits, not case-by-case calculations (applied to entrepreneurship by Melé, 2009). |
- **Stakeholder theory (Freeman, 1984):** businesses owe obligations not only to shareholders but to all affected parties — employees, customers, suppliers, communities, environment.
- **Case — Nigeria's "pure water" (sachet water) industry:** worth over ₦200bn yearly and provides affordable water, but NAFDAC investigations repeatedly find unregistered operators failing microbiological safety standards, and some falsify registration numbers. The utilitarian defence ("affordable water is better than no water") is ethically insufficient when the water is unsafe — a duty of care (deontological) is violated.

### 7.2 Sustainability beyond greenwashing
- **Triple Bottom Line (TBL)** — Elkington (1997): **People, Planet, Profit**. Elkington himself (2018) later criticised how it became a reporting exercise — a "sustainability accounting placebo" — without driving systemic change.
- **Circular economy:** design waste out of production (reuse, repair, remanufacture, recycle) instead of "take–make–dispose". Critique (Gregson et al., 2015): it optimistically assumes technical design can fix what are really political-economy problems.
- Nigeria: urgent environmental problems (Niger Delta oil spillage; Lagos ~10,000 tonnes of solid waste daily; Middle Belt deforestation) are also opportunities — e.g. **Wecyclers** (Lagos plastic recycling), **Rensource** (off-grid solar).
- **"Profit with purpose" — critical lens:** Henderson (2020) argues voluntary CSR cannot fix systemic market failures; Friedman (1970) argued the social responsibility of business is to increase profits — fiduciary pressures can override social commitments. Treat "profit with purpose" as an ideal with real structural constraints.

### 7.3 The future of work (Nigeria and global)
Three intersecting trends:
1. **Automation:** Nigerian employment is concentrated in agriculture and the informal economy, where full automation is uneconomic in the near term — but AI tools (automated customer service, AI credit scoring) are already reducing formal-sector jobs in banking, call centres, and administration.
2. **Gig economy:** platform-mediated, on-demand, short-term work (Bolt, Uber Eats, Jumia Food; Upwork, Toptal). Offers flexibility and income diversification but systematically denies employment protections (minimum wage, health insurance, pensions). Policy dilemma: protecting gig workers may reduce platforms' willingness to engage them; leaving them unprotected reproduces precarity.
3. **Remote work / BPO:** COVID-19 opened global labour markets to Nigerian professionals — English proficiency, competitive labour costs, and growing broadband make business process outsourcing a genuine opportunity.

## Lecture EP15: Business Model Development

### What is a business model?
- A business model describes the rationale of **how an organisation creates, delivers, and captures value**. Three fundamental ideas: **creation, delivery, capture**.
  - *Create* products/services that meet customer requirements, expectations, and quality.
  - *Deliver* them at the right time, at an affordable price, through the right channels.
  - *Capture* value — check that you are truly creating value that satisfies human wants and needs.
- A business model can also be viewed as **a plan for satisfying human wants and needs** built from **four interlocking parts**:
  1. **The offering**
  2. **The customers**
  3. **The infrastructure**
  4. **Financial viability**
- The central focus of any business model is **value creation** → which gives **competitive advantage** → which allows you to **satisfy customer needs and wants**.

### Importance of a business model (know these four)
1. **Clarity and focus** — a well-defined model clarifies the purpose and operations of the business; it is a roadmap that aligns all parts of the business toward a common goal and ensures resources are well allocated and utilised.
2. **Strategic planning** — it helps a business formulate, communicate, and execute strategy by giving a clear understanding of how the company intends to create and capture value in the marketplace.
3. **Resource allocation / mobilisation** — by identifying key activities, resources, and partnerships, the model supports efficient allocation and use of resources.
4. **Innovation** — a good model encourages innovation by deepening the understanding of customer needs and market dynamics.

### Steps in developing a successful business model
(No universally accepted method, but these steps help.)
1. **Identify the target audience/customers** — segment the market clearly and pinpoint the categories of customers you intend to serve.
2. **Define the value proposition** — why should customers choose your product over competitors'? Identify your **core competence**. Supporting tools: **environmental analysis** (environmental scanning, benchmarking, scenario development, market segmentation, forecasting).
3. **Determine revenue streams** — how the organisation will make money (subscriptions, one-off sales, etc.); links to capital structure.
4. **Outline key activities and resources** — detail essential operations, capacity, technology, and partnerships needed to deliver; decide channels (online vs physical distribution).
5. **Estimate the cost structure** — identify fixed and variable costs, operating and overhead expenses, so pricing is sustainable, affordable, and yields reasonable margins.
6. **Testing and iteration** — a business model is basically an **assumption**; validate it with surveys, prototypes, or pilot programmes before full-scale deployment, repeating steps until the model aligns with strategic objectives.

### Constraints (shortcomings) of business models
1. **Dynamic, unpredictable environment** — the business environment changes constantly, so no model is 100% accurate.
2. **Dependency on technology** — models rely on technology, which carries risks of disruption and failure.
3. **Intense competition** — in highly competitive industries a model may not deliver expected results (pricing pressure, thin margins).
4. **Revenue uncertainty** — economic volatility and unpredictable customer preferences make revenue hard to capture in the model.
5. **Longer sales cycles in B2B models** — corporate buyers have complex, slow decision processes, so results are not immediate.

### Frameworks
- Two popular frameworks: the **Business Model Canvas** and the **Lean Canvas**.
- (Class assignment was to write notes on the Business Model Canvas — likely exam material. The canvas has nine building blocks: customer segments, value propositions, channels, customer relationships, revenue streams, key resources, key activities, key partnerships, cost structure — with cost structure and revenue streams the most commonly underspecified, per the ENT 1000 handout.)

## Lecture EP16: Marketing Research and Strategy Development

### What is marketing research?
- **Marketing research is a technique used to systematically collect and analyse data to facilitate informed decision-making.**
- It covers: **market conditions**, **consumer preferences**, and the **competitive landscape**.
- Under marketing research you must understand:
  - **Consumer behaviour** — demographic, geographic, and behavioural characteristics and perceptions of consumers.
  - **Market dynamism** — every market behaves differently; research shows how to enter and operate in each.

### How marketing research is conducted
- **Surveys** — collect feedback from consumers and also from **retailers and wholesalers** (firms often reach consumers through distribution channels, including agents and brokers).
- **Focus groups** — a panel of about **6–10 people** set up to examine a specific problem and suggest solutions.
- **Interviews** — one-on-one interactions with consumers or retailers (retailers are close to consumers, so their feedback is valuable).
- **Observational studies.**

### How it supports organisational goals
- **Customer satisfaction** — needs and wants are identified and addressed, building the relationship between producer and customer.
- **Improved business performance** — knowing your problems and your competitors gives competitive advantage and raises profitability.

### Key reasons for studying marketing research
1. **Valuable insights** — how to enter a market (existing or new) and how to launch a **new product**. Remember the **product life cycle: introduction → growth → maturity → decline**; at introduction, run a **SWOT analysis** (Strengths, Weaknesses, Opportunities, Threats) and get **pricing** right.
2. **Consumer-centric approach** — categorise consumers into groups by their different needs so your product matches each group.
3. **Forecasting and planning** — supports projections. Levels of planning: **strategic** (about 5 years), **tactical** (about 1 year), **operational** (daily). Objectives break long-term plans down into day-to-day activities.
4. **Competitive advantage** — planning based on research gives you an edge over rivals.

### Methods of marketing research
- Two broad categories: **quantitative and qualitative**.
- Primary techniques: **focus groups** (6–10 people), **one-to-one interviews**, **surveys and questionnaires** (open-ended or close-ended).

### Steps for conducting marketing research (know this sequence)
1. **Identify the problem** — the starting point of all research.
2. **Articulate research objectives** — objectives in line with the problem guide data collection and analysis (e.g. a pricing problem → objectives about competitive pricing).
3. **Define the sample** — a representative sample size so results are not biased; never just one or two people.
4. **Collect data** — interviews, focus groups, surveys/questionnaires.
5. **Analyse the results** — tools include **Excel, SPSS, EViews**.
6. **Interpret the analysis** — so that even a layman can understand the findings.
7. **Write the research report** — findings in a structured format.
8. **Decision-making** — the final step; the decision gives the roadmap forward.

### Five market research tips
1. **Define clear objectives** — break goals down into objectives aligned with the business's priorities, mission, and vision.
2. **Know your target customers** — their demographics, behaviour, and preferences, to improve marketing effectiveness.
3. **Segment the market** — divide it into areas to tailor strategies and optimise the product offering.
4. **Analyse competitors** — those "dragging the market" (market share, profit, advantage) with you.
5. **Engage with customers** — personal engagement and feedback.

### Why does every business need market research?
Every business — big firm, partnership, or one-man business — needs it, to:
1. Understand **competitors**;
2. Understand **market trends**;
3. Validate and project a **new business idea**;
4. Find opportunities to **enter new markets**;
5. Gain insight on **launching new products**;
6. Learn **how to operate** in a given market.

### Segmentation, Targeting, and Positioning (STP)
- **Segmentation** — dividing a market into different groups according to customers' different needs (demographic, geographic, behavioural).
- **Targeting** — after segmenting, choose the particular market that will give you competitive advantage over rivals.
- **Positioning** — planting the product or brand **in the mind of the customers**, so they become **loyal customers**: they are *aware* of the product → they *purchase* it → they *use* it → they *tell others* who are not yet aware.

### Market entry strategies
- **Meaning:** the strategies a business uses to enter different markets — including online "spaces" (search engines, social media, email, WhatsApp, Facebook, Instagram). Choose strategies that ensure efficient resource allocation, competitive positioning, and market success.
- **Benefits:**
  1. **Revenue growth** — new markets bring new revenue.
  2. **Sales potential** — because you have segmented, targeted, and positioned the brand.
  3. **Market diversification** — presence in multiple markets reduces business risk.
  4. **Competitive advantage** — you lead the market and customers become loyal to the brand.
- Practical tip from the lecture: advertise **where your customers actually are** — if most of your customers are on WhatsApp, put your adverts on WhatsApp, not Facebook.
- **Key components:** market research; **mode of entry**; value proposition.
- **Four key modes of entry:**
  1. **Exporting** — shipping your product or service directly to customers in another country.
  2. **Licensing** — allowing another company to manufacture or sell the product under your brand name; both parties profit.
  3. **Joint ventures** — teaming up with a local or international company to share resources, expertise, and profit.
  4. **Franchising** — allowing others to run the business ("run the show") under your brand name.

### Steps to implement a market entry strategy
1. Conduct thorough **market research**.
2. Define the **value proposition**.
3. Choose the **mode of entry**.
4. Develop a **detailed plan** — strategic, tactical, and operational planning, linked to the **marketing mix (4 Ps: Product, Price, Place, Promotion)**.
5. **Allocate resources** (human, financial, material).
6. **Establish local partnerships** — you cannot run the show alone; partners may bring capital and capability.
7. **Implement** the marketing strategies.
8. **Monitor and measure performance** — keep adapting and improving.

### Competitive analysis
- **Definition:** the process of **gathering information about your competitors** and using it to **identify their strengths and weaknesses**, then developing strategies that improve your own business and give you competitive advantage.
- **How to conduct it:**
  1. **Identify your competitors** — direct and indirect.
  2. **Analyse the market.**
  3. **Create a competitor framework.**
  4. **Research your competitors.**
  5. **Assess their strengths and weaknesses** — their weaknesses become your opportunities.
  6. **Identify opportunities and threats** — this yields your competitive advantage.
